Deepfake detection is the process of using artificial intelligence models to determine whether a piece of media -- an image, video, or audio clip -- has been synthetically generated or manipulated. Modern deepfake detection systems analyze pixel-level artifacts, temporal inconsistencies, spectral features, and semantic anomalies that are imperceptible to the human eye but statistically identifiable by trained neural networks. The Decentralized Fake Proof Network (DFPN) employs four specialized detection models that together cover face manipulation, AI-generated imagery, video tampering, and voice cloning, achieving accuracy rates between 97.2% and 99.8% depending on the modality.

## What Are the Main Types of Deepfakes?

Deepfakes fall into four broad categories, each requiring a distinct detection approach:

1. **Face manipulation** -- Face swaps, reenactment, and attribute editing applied to photographs or video frames. Tools like FaceSwap, DeepFaceLab, and first-order motion models are commonly used.
2. **Fully AI-generated images** -- Entire images synthesized from scratch by generative adversarial networks (GANs) or diffusion models such as Stable Diffusion, Midjourney, and DALL-E.
3. **Video deepfakes** -- Temporal sequences where face swaps or reenactment are applied frame-by-frame, introducing subtle inter-frame inconsistencies.
4. **Voice cloning and synthetic speech** -- Audio generated by text-to-speech systems or real-time voice conversion tools that replicate a target speaker's vocal characteristics.

Each category leaves different forensic traces, which is why a single detection model cannot reliably cover all threats.

## How Does Face Manipulation Detection Work?

DFPN uses an **EfficientNet-B4** backbone trained with the Self-Blended Images (SBI) strategy for face manipulation detection. The SBI approach creates training data by blending a face with a transformed version of itself, forcing the model to learn the boundary artifacts that all face-swap methods produce rather than overfitting to any single generation technique.

The detection pipeline operates as follows:

1. **Face extraction** -- MTCNN or RetinaFace locates and crops facial regions from the input image.
2. **Preprocessing** -- The cropped face is resized to 380x380 pixels and normalized.
3. **Feature extraction** -- The EfficientNet-B4 encoder extracts a 1792-dimensional feature vector that captures blending boundaries, compression artifacts, and color inconsistencies.
4. **Classification** -- A fully connected head outputs a probability score between 0 (authentic) and 1 (manipulated).

This model achieves **97.2% accuracy on the FaceForensics++ (FF++) benchmark** at high-quality compression (c23) and **96.4% on the raw FaceForensics++ dataset**, making it one of the most reliable face forensics classifiers available.

## How Are AI-Generated Images Detected?

Detecting fully AI-generated images requires a different approach because there is no original face to compare against. DFPN uses a **CLIP-ViT-L/14** model fine-tuned for binary classification of real versus synthetic images.

CLIP (Contrastive Language-Image Pre-training) learns a joint embedding space for images and text by training on hundreds of millions of image-text pairs. This broad visual understanding gives it strong generalization across different generators. The detection model fine-tunes CLIP's vision transformer with a classification head that distinguishes authentic photographs from GAN or diffusion outputs.

Key performance metrics:

| Generator family | Accuracy |
|---|---|
| ProGAN | 99.8% |
| StyleGAN2 | 98.6% |
| Stable Diffusion v1.5 | 95.3% |
| Cross-generator average | 96.1% |

The model processes images in approximately **50ms on GPU** and **500ms on CPU**, making it suitable for real-time verification workflows.

## How Does Video Deepfake Detection Differ from Image Detection?

Video deepfakes introduce a temporal dimension that still images lack. While a single manipulated frame might pass inspection, the sequence of frames often reveals inconsistencies in head pose, lighting direction, blinking patterns, and lip synchronization.

DFPN's video analysis module uses a **temporal convolutional network (CNN)** that processes sequences of frames rather than individual images. The pipeline:

1. **Frame sampling** -- Extracts keyframes at regular intervals (typically every 5th frame) to balance coverage and efficiency.
2. **Per-frame feature extraction** -- Each frame passes through a convolutional backbone to produce spatial features.
3. **Temporal aggregation** -- A 1D temporal convolution layer analyzes how features change across frames, detecting unnatural jumps and inconsistencies.
4. **Sequence classification** -- The aggregated representation is classified as authentic or manipulated.

This temporal approach catches artifacts that frame-by-frame analysis misses, such as flickering around face boundaries, inconsistent eye-gaze trajectories, and unnatural head movement smoothing.

## How Is Voice Cloning Detected?

Voice cloning detection is arguably the fastest-growing need in deepfake forensics. Modern text-to-speech systems can clone a voice from as little as 3 seconds of reference audio, making phone-based fraud and misinformation campaigns increasingly accessible.

DFPN employs **wav2vec 2.0 (XLSR-53)**, a self-supervised speech representation model pre-trained on 56,000 hours of multilingual audio. The model is fine-tuned for binary classification on the ASVspoof 2021 dataset, achieving **99.2% accuracy** with an equal error rate (EER) of 0.8%.

Processing speed benchmarks:

- **GPU inference**: ~200ms per 10-second clip
- **CPU inference**: ~2 seconds per 10-second clip
- **Maximum supported clip length**: 60 seconds (longer clips are segmented)

The model detects artifacts across the frequency spectrum, including unnatural formant transitions, missing micro-prosodic features, and spectral smoothing that characterizes neural vocoders.

## Why Does Multi-Model Consensus Matter?

A single detection model, no matter how accurate, has blind spots. Different generation techniques leave different artifacts, and adversarial attacks can target specific model architectures. DFPN addresses this through **multi-model consensus** -- submitting media to multiple independent detection workers and aggregating their results.

Here is how single-model and multi-model approaches compare:

| Criterion | Single model | Multi-model consensus (DFPN) |
|---|---|---|
| Accuracy ceiling | Limited by one architecture | Combines strengths of multiple architectures |
| Adversarial robustness | Vulnerable to targeted attacks | Attacker must defeat multiple independent models |
| False positive rate | Model-dependent | Reduced through agreement thresholds |
| Transparency | Opaque single score | Multiple independent scores with on-chain audit trail |
| Latency | 50ms - 500ms | 200ms - 2s (parallel execution across workers) |

In DFPN's protocol, a detection request is routed to multiple workers who run their analysis independently and submit results via a **commit-reveal protocol**. Workers first commit a hash of their result, then reveal the actual classification. This prevents workers from copying each other's answers and ensures genuine independent analysis.

The final consensus is computed on-chain, and clients can configure their own agreement thresholds -- for example, requiring 3 out of 4 workers to agree before accepting a classification.

## What Accuracy Can You Expect in Production?

Lab benchmarks and production accuracy often diverge because real-world media comes with compression artifacts, varying resolutions, and mixed content. DFPN's models are benchmarked on standard academic datasets, but the multi-model consensus mechanism helps bridge the gap between lab and production performance.

Summary of DFPN benchmark results:

| Model | Architecture | Dataset | Accuracy |
|---|---|---|---|
| Face manipulation | EfficientNet-B4 SBI | FF++ (c23) | 97.2% |
| AI-generated images | CLIP-ViT-L/14 | ProGAN | 99.8% |
| Video deepfakes | Temporal CNN | FaceForensics++ | 96.4% |
| Voice cloning | wav2vec 2.0 XLSR-53 | ASVspoof 2021 | 99.2% |

These numbers represent per-model accuracy. When combined through multi-model consensus with appropriate thresholds, the effective false positive rate drops significantly because uncorrelated errors across independent models are unlikely to align.

## How Do Detection Models Stay Current?

The deepfake arms race means that new generation techniques emerge regularly. DFPN's decentralized architecture addresses this by allowing **model developers to publish updated models** that workers can adopt. The protocol includes:

- **Model registration** -- New models are registered on-chain with metadata describing their capabilities and benchmark results.
- **Worker adoption** -- Workers choose which models to run based on expected rewards and their hardware capabilities.
- **Performance tracking** -- On-chain records of worker accuracy allow clients to identify the most reliable workers and models.
- **Economic incentives** -- Workers running more accurate models earn higher rewards, creating a natural selection pressure toward better detection.

This creates a self-improving ecosystem where economic incentives align with detection quality.
